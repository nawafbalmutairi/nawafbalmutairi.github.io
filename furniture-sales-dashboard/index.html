Imputation Approach & Justification
Method selection supported by Little & Rubin (2002)
Methodology
Method: Mean imputation for continuous variables (Sales, Discount) and mode imputation for categorical variables. As Little & Rubin (2002) suggest, imputation is viable when missing data is below 5% — the 212 missing values represent exactly 5.0% of total cells across the two affected columns.

Mean imputation preserves the overall distribution by substituting missing values with the mean of available cases. Mode imputation maintains the distribution shape for categorical variables by replacing missing data with the most frequent category.

Result: After cleaning, the dataset contains 0 missing values across all 21 columns and 2,121 rows — confirmed suitable for analysis without risk of biased conclusions (Bennett, 2001).

Interpretation: Sales and profits show clear seasonal patterns — Q4 (November–December) consistently delivers the highest revenue across all four years, driven by holiday demand. However, high sales do not always translate to high profits; several months show positive revenue but negative profit, indicating that discounting and operational costs erode margins.

Implications: Businesses should time inventory build-up and marketing campaigns for the Q3–Q4 period to capitalise on this predictable seasonal pattern. Resource allocation and pricing strategies should be reviewed to ensure the seasonal sales spike also translates into actual profitability, not just top-line revenue growth.

Interpretation: The scatter plot reveals no distinct linear pattern between discount and profit. Transactions at 0% discount cluster in the positive-profit zone, while discounts above 40% almost universally produce losses. The discount band analysis confirms this: 0% discounts generate $55.5K profit from 787 orders, while the 41–70% band loses -$23.6K from just 207 orders.

Implications: Simply modifying discounts is inadequate for reliably impacting profitability. Businesses should adopt segment-based targeted discounting rather than blanket promotions. Additional factors — product margins, customer buying patterns, competitive strategy — must be considered alongside discount rates when making pricing decisions.

Interpretation: High sales do not always mean high profits. Texas ($58K sales, -$9.7K profit), Illinois ($27K sales, -$8.6K profit), and Pennsylvania ($39K sales, -$7.1K profit) are among the largest revenue generators yet all operate at a significant net loss.
